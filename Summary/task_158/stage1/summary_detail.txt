The summary of key features of successful candidates for the Expert Mechanism on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples (EMRIP) in the UNHRC reveals several trends and characteristics that may guide future candidate selection processes:

1. **Age Trend**: The shortlisted candidates exhibit a wide age range, from 42 to 66 years old. This suggests that both experienced professionals and those with a longer career history are valued, indicating a preference for candidates with substantial experience in the field.

2. **Language Abilities**: All successful candidates demonstrated a high level of English proficiency, which is crucial given the international nature of the UNHRC and the need for effective communication in global forums. This highlights the importance of English language skills for this mandate.

3. **Gender Balance**: The shortlisted candidates include both males and females, with a slight prevalence of female candidates. This indicates a balanced consideration of gender in the selection process, although the specific proportion of each gender could be further analyzed to ensure equality.

4. **Nationality and Regional Representation**: The successful candidates hail from diverse regions, including Asia, with representatives from China, the Philippines, and Bangladesh. This suggests that candidates from various national and regional backgrounds are considered, emphasizing the importance of regional representation and diversity.

5. **Legal Tradition**: The candidates' backgrounds in law and international relations, particularly in international law and human rights, seem to be advantageous. This is evident from the educational and professional backgrounds of the candidates, who have extensive experience in legal fields relevant to indigenous rights.

6. **Location of Education**: The diversity in the location of education, including universities in both OECD countries and the global south, is evident among the successful candidates. This indicates that a broad educational background, encompassing various global contexts, is beneficial. Candidates who have studied in multiple regions or internationally recognized institutions appear to be favored.

In conclusion, the selection process for EMRIP members in the UNHRC seems to favor candidates with a broad range of experience, high English proficiency, balanced gender representation, diverse regional backgrounds, strong legal and international relations backgrounds, and education from varied global settings. These factors collectively contribute to the selection of well-rounded and capable candidates who can effectively represent and advocate for the rights of indigenous peoples on an international platform.

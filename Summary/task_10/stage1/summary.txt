The successful candidates for the Expert Mechanism on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples (EMRIP) from Latin American and Caribbean States in the United Nations Human Rights Council exhibit several key features that align with the mandate and requirements of the position. These features include:

1. **Strong Linguistic Proficiency**: Successful candidates demonstrated a high level of proficiency in Spanish, which is crucial for effectively communicating and engaging with indigenous communities in the Latin American and Caribbean region. Additionally, candidates with high or intermediate English levels were favored, reflecting the need for international communication within the UN framework.

2. **Diverse Professional Backgrounds**: The shortlisted candidates possessed a mix of legal, academic, and government backgrounds. This diversity ensures a comprehensive understanding of both legal frameworks and practical implementation of indigenous rights. Candidates with experience in law-related positions were particularly noted, highlighting the importance of legal expertise in addressing indigenous rights issues.

3. **Relevant Current Job Roles**: Successful candidates were currently engaged in roles that are directly related to law, academia, or government, reflecting active involvement in areas pertinent to indigenous rights. This active engagement ensures that candidates bring current, practical knowledge and insights to the EMRIP.

4. **Age and Experience**: While there is no strict age criterion, the shortlisted candidates ranged from middle to late adulthood, suggesting a preference for individuals with substantial experience and maturity in dealing with complex human rights issues.

5. **Nationality and Cultural Relevance**: Candidates from various countries within the Latin American and Caribbean region were selected, ensuring a broad representation and understanding of the diverse indigenous communities within these states.

In summary, the selection process favored candidates who demonstrated strong linguistic skills in Spanish and English, possessed diverse and relevant professional backgrounds, were actively engaged in pertinent roles, and brought a wealth of experience and cultural relevance to the table. These criteria ensure that the selected candidates are well-equipped to effectively advocate for and support the rights of indigenous peoples within the UN framework.

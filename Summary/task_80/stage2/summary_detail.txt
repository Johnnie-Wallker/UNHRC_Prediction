This suggests that a broad geographic representation is valued, with no single region or country dominating the selections.

5. **Legal Tradition and Education**: Candidates with backgrounds in law, international relations, and human rights are prevalent. Education in prestigious institutions, both within and outside the OECD countries, is common among the successful candidates, indicating that the quality and relevance of education are crucial.

6. **Diversity of Education Location**: The successful candidates have studied in various locations, including both OECD countries and regions like the Caribbean and the Middle East. This diversity in educational exposure seems to be a positive attribute, suggesting that candidates who have experienced different educational and cultural environments are highly regarded.

In conclusion, the selection process for this mandate favors candidates who are experienced, multilingual, and have a strong educational and professional background in human rights and international law. The geographic and gender diversity among the candidates is also a significant factor, with a preference for those who have studied and worked in diverse international settings.
